Monte Carlo Sampling from a Beta-Distribution Posterior

In the previous session, we encountered the Beta distribution and considered how to determine the parameters of a Beta posterior distribution for allele frequencies.

For review, if \(q\), the frequency of the 1 allele in a poplution has a Beta prior with parameters \(a\) and \(b\), we say: \[ q \sim \mathrm{Beta}(a, b) \] And, if \(x\) counts the number of 1 alleles at a marker found in our sample of size \(N\) gene copies from the population (for example, of elephants). Then the posterior probability distribution for \(q\) is also a Beta distribution: \[ p(q|x) \propto p(q|x)p(q) = q^{x + a - 1}(1-q)^{N-x+b - 1}. \] Such a distribution can be recognized as a Beta distribution with parameters \(x+a\) and \(N - x + b\): \[ q|x \sim \mathrm{Beta}(x+a, N-x+b). \]

In the previous session, you wrote a function to compute the posterior distributions of the allele frequencies in \(K\) different populations using a sample of individual genotypes, and a vector telling us which population each of those individuals belonged to.

As we will see, that is not the endpoint of a genetic mixture analysis, but we will pause here and consider Monte Carlo sampling in the context of having a posterior distribution for the frequency \(q\) of the 1 allele in a single population.

Imagine we are estimating the frequency of the 1 allele when we have sampled 50 diploid individuals from a population. This means we have a sample of 100 gene copies. For the purposes of estimating \(q\), in a population that is in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, this is similar to sampling \(N=100\) haploid individuals.

Suppose 30 of those gene copies were of allelic type 1 and the remaining 70 were of allelic type 0.

Further, assume a uniform prior, (i.e., a \(\mathrm{Beta}(1,1)\) prior distribution) on \(q\), the frequency of the 1 allele.

As we saw earlier, such a setup yields a posterior distribution for the allele frequency that is a \(\mathrm{Beta}(31, 71)\) distribution.

Various summaries of this posterior (posterior mean, posterior median, credible intervals, etc.) are analytically avaiable; however, we will practice obtaining them (and, further below, more elaborate summaries) using Monte Carlo sampling.

A sample from a Beta distribution can be obtained from Rs rbeta() function. Thus the following gives a Monte Carlo sample of size \(n = 1000\) from a \(\mathrm{Beta}(31,71)\) distribution:

S <- rbeta(1000, 31, 71)

Further exercises

We have the posterior distribution of the frequency of the 1 allele, but we might be more interested in the frequency of individuals with the genotype 11; i.e., those diploid individuals that have two copies of the 1 allele. Assume that the population is in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium. If a population is in Hardy Weinberg equilibrium and allele 1 is at a frequency of \(q\), then the expected frequency of 11 homozygotes is \(q^2\).

  1. Use a Monte Carlo sample of size \(n = 1,000,000\) of the allele frequency to make an approximation (as a histogram) of the posterior distribution of the frequency of 11 homozygotes.
S <- rbeta(1e6, 31, 71)
hist(S ^ 2, breaks = seq(-0.01, 1.01, by = 0.01))

  1. Find the posterior probability that the frequency of 11 homozygotes (assuming H-W equilibrium) is greater than 0.15.
mean(rbeta(1e6, 31, 71) ^ 2 > 0.15)
[1] 0.037589
  1. Given the posterior for the frequency of the 1 allele, and assuming H-W equilibrium, evaluate the posterior probability that a new and different sample of 10 individuals from this population will not include a 11 homozygote.
S <- rbeta(1e6, 31, 71)

# for the sample to not include a 11 homozygote, that means that each time we
# sampled an individual it was not a 11.  The probability that an individual is
# 11 is q^2 so the probability that it is not 11 is 1 - q^2.  The probability
# that all 10 are not 11 is thus (1 - q^2)^10. So...
mean( (1 - S ^ 2) ^ 10)
[1] 0.3863345

These final three exercises illustrate one of the convenient aspects of Monte Carlo sampling from a posterior. If you are interested in the posterior distribution of some function of the variable you have the posterior for, you can evaluate that directly by sampling the posterior you have in hand. This is often much easier than trying to analytically derive the posterior for your function via methods for finding the distribution of transformed random variables.
